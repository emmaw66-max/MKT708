Step 0 — Load everything from prior weeks

import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns
sns.set_theme(style="whitegrid")

df  = pd.read_csv("retail_clean.csv")
df["InvoiceDate"] = pd.to_datetime(df["InvoiceDate"])
df["LineRevenue"] = df["Quantity"] * df["Price"]

rfm = pd.read_csv("rfm_segments.csv")   # from Week 3
print("Customers:", rfm['Customer ID'].nunique())

Part 1 — Simple projected CLV by segment

State your assumptions out loud. CLV is only as credible as its inputs. Declare them at the top of the cell so a reader can challenge them. We use a simple, transparent formula:

CLV = annual revenue per customer × profit margin × expected lifespan (years), where expected lifespan = 1 ÷ annual churn rate.
1

Set assumptions

MARGIN = 0.10        # assume 10% profit margin on revenue
ANNUAL_CHURN = 0.30  # assume 30% of customers lapse each year
EXPECTED_LIFESPAN = 1 / ANNUAL_CHURN   # ~3.33 years
print("Expected lifespan (years):", round(EXPECTED_LIFESPAN, 2))
2

Annualize each customer's revenue

The dataset spans about two years, so convert total spend to an annual rate before projecting.

span_days = (df["InvoiceDate"].max() - df["InvoiceDate"].min()).days
years_of_data = span_days / 365
print("Years of data:", round(years_of_data, 2))

cust_rev = df.groupby("Customer ID")["LineRevenue"].sum().reset_index()
cust_rev["annual_revenue"] = cust_rev["LineRevenue"] / years_of_data

clv = cust_rev.merge(rfm[["Customer ID","Segment"]], on="Customer ID", how="left")
clv["CLV"] = clv["annual_revenue"] * MARGIN * EXPECTED_LIFESPAN
clv.head()
3

CLV summarized by segment

clv_by_seg = clv.groupby("Segment").agg(
    customers   = ("Customer ID", "count"),
    avg_CLV     = ("CLV", "mean"),
    total_CLV   = ("CLV", "sum"),
).reset_index().sort_values("total_CLV", ascending=False).round(0)
clv_by_seg
Sensitivity note (nice to include): re-run with MARGIN = 0.15 or ANNUAL_CHURN = 0.40 and mention how much the CLV ranking shifts. It rarely changes which segments lead, which is a reassuring robustness point.

Part 2 — Cohort retention matrix

Group customers by the month of their first purchase (their cohort), then track what fraction are still active in each later month.

1

Assign cohort and period

df["order_month"]  = df["InvoiceDate"].dt.to_period("M")
df["cohort"]       = df.groupby("Customer ID")["InvoiceDate"].transform("min").dt.to_period("M")

# months since the cohort started
df["period_number"] = (df["order_month"].astype("int64") - df["cohort"].astype("int64"))
2

Build the retention matrix

cohort_counts = (df.groupby(["cohort","period_number"])["Customer ID"]
                   .nunique().reset_index())
matrix = cohort_counts.pivot(index="cohort", columns="period_number", values="Customer ID")

cohort_sizes = matrix.iloc[:, 0]
retention = matrix.divide(cohort_sizes, axis=0)   # share of each cohort retained
retention.round(2).head(10)
3

Heatmap of retention

plt.figure(figsize=(13,8))
sns.heatmap(retention, annot=False, cmap="YlGnBu", vmin=0, vmax=0.5,
            cbar_kws={"label":"Retention rate"})
plt.title("Cohort Retention by First-Purchase Month")
plt.xlabel("Months since first purchase"); plt.ylabel("Cohort (first-purchase month)")
plt.show()
4

Average retention curve

avg_curve = retention.mean(axis=0)
plt.figure(figsize=(9,5))
avg_curve.plot(marker="o")
plt.title("Average Retention Curve (all cohorts)")
plt.xlabel("Months since first purchase"); plt.ylabel("Average retention rate")
plt.ylim(0, avg_curve.max()*1.1)
plt.show()
What you'll see: a steep drop after month 0 — most first-time buyers don't return the next month — then a small, loyal tail that persists. That tail is where retention spend pays off.

Part 3 — Identify churned customers

Define churn with a recency threshold: a customer is "churned" if they haven't purchased in more than N days as of the snapshot.

CHURN_DAYS = 180   # assumption: no purchase in 6+ months = churned

snapshot = df["InvoiceDate"].max() + pd.Timedelta(days=1)
last_purchase = df.groupby("Customer ID")["InvoiceDate"].max()
recency_days  = (snapshot - last_purchase).dt.days

churn = recency_days.reset_index()
churn.columns = ["Customer ID", "recency_days"]
churn["churned"] = churn["recency_days"] > CHURN_DAYS

print(churn["churned"].value_counts())
print("Churn rate:", round(churn["churned"].mean(), 3))

Part 4 — Priority matrix: churn risk vs. revenue

Combine churn risk and revenue at the segment level. The top-left quadrant — high churn risk and high revenue — is where to focus retention dollars.

seg = (churn.merge(rfm[["Customer ID","Segment"]], on="Customer ID")
            .merge(cust_rev[["Customer ID","annual_revenue"]], on="Customer ID"))

prio = seg.groupby("Segment").agg(
    churn_rate     = ("churned", "mean"),
    avg_annual_rev = ("annual_revenue", "mean"),
    customers      = ("Customer ID", "count"),
).reset_index()
prio
plt.figure(figsize=(9,6.5))
plt.scatter(prio["churn_rate"], prio["avg_annual_rev"],
            s=prio["customers"]/3, alpha=0.6, edgecolor="black")
for _, r in prio.iterrows():
    plt.annotate(r["Segment"], (r["churn_rate"], r["avg_annual_rev"]),
                 xytext=(5,5), textcoords="offset points", fontsize=9)
plt.axvline(prio["churn_rate"].median(), ls="--", color="gray")
plt.axhline(prio["avg_annual_rev"].median(), ls="--", color="gray")
plt.title("Priority Matrix: Churn Risk vs. Revenue by Segment\n(bubble size = number of customers)")
plt.xlabel("Churn rate (higher = more at risk)")
plt.ylabel("Average annual revenue per customer (£)")
plt.show()
Reading the quadrants: top-right = high value & high risk (defend now); top-left = high value & loyal (nurture); bottom-right = low value & leaving (let go cheaply); bottom-left = low value & loyal (maintain efficiently).
