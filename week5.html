Half a page, written last. The 3–4 most important findings and your headline recommendations. A busy reader should get the whole story here.

2. Dataset Overview

What the data is, the cleaning you did (cite row counts from AA1), and the customer base after cleaning.

3. Customer Segmentation

Your RFM method and the segment profile table from AA2. Lead with the revenue-concentration story.

4. Customer Value Analysis

CLV by segment from AA3, with assumptions stated explicitly. Which segments are worth the most over time?

5. Retention Analysis

The cohort matrix and retention curve. Where do customers drop off, and which cohorts hold?

6. Strategic Recommendations by Segment

The core of the grade: 5+ concrete, segment-level recommendations. Each ties a finding to an action and an expected payoff.

Honest caveats (RFM is backward-looking; CLV assumptions are simple; one retailer, one region) and what you'd analyze next.

Deliverable 2 — Executive Summary Slides (5–7 slides)

A standalone deck — a reader should get the findings and recommendations without the report. One chart per slide, no output tables.

A 6-slide skeleton that works

  1. Title + the one-sentence takeaway (e.g., "A fifth of customers drive most revenue — here's how to protect them").
  2. The opportunity — revenue concentration chart.
  3. Who the customers are — segment share (customers vs. revenue).
  4. What they're worth — CLV by segment.
  5. Who's at risk — priority matrix (churn vs. revenue).
  6. Recommendations — 3–4 prioritized actions by segment.
Design discipline: one idea per slide, a headline that states the insight (not "RFM Results"), and the chart as evidence. If a slide needs a table to make sense, it's trying to do too much.

Deliverable 3 — Professional Reflection (optional · 1–2 pages · +5% bonus)

Connect at least two course methods to your professional context (your internship or current role). Address three things:

  1. Transfer: how the analytical logic could apply to a real decision or dataset in your role — e.g., supplier segmentation, vendor lifetime value, or category spend patterns instead of retail customers.
  2. One limitation of a method that would matter in your setting (e.g., RFM ignores contract terms; CLV assumptions are fragile for B2B relationships).
  3. One question the methods raise that you'd like to explore further in your career.
No forced analogies needed. The syllabus is explicit: the goal is critical thinking about transferability, not pretending a fintech internship is identical to a UK gift retailer. Thoughtful "here's where it doesn't map" reasoning scores well.
